# Product Discovery and Problem Framing

## Discovery Before Delivery
Product discovery is the work of deciding what to build before committing to
delivery. Its goal is to reduce the risk of building something nobody wants. Teams
that skip discovery often ship features that are technically sound but solve the
wrong problem.

## Framing the Problem
A well-framed problem statement is specific, evidence-based, and does not smuggle in
a solution. Compare "Users need a bulk-export button" (a solution) with "Users who
manage more than 50 records cannot get their data out of the product without manual
copying, which takes hours and causes errors" (a problem). The second invites many
possible solutions and states the pain and its cost.

## Jobs To Be Done
The Jobs-To-Be-Done lens asks what job a user "hires" the product to do. It focuses
on the underlying goal and the circumstances, not the demographic. Framing needs as
jobs ("When I onboard a new client, I want to gather their documents quickly, so I
can start work sooner") keeps the team anchored on outcomes.

## The Four Big Risks
Marty Cagan describes four risks discovery must address: value risk (will people
use or buy it?), usability risk (can they figure out how to use it?), feasibility
risk (can we build it with the time, skills, and technology we have?), and business
viability risk (does it work for our business, legal, and brand?). A PRD should show
these risks have been considered.

## Techniques
Common discovery techniques include customer interviews, surveys, analytics review,
competitive analysis, prototype testing, and small experiments. The aim is to gather
enough evidence to move an assumption from a guess to a validated belief before
expensive engineering begins.

## Validate Assumptions Cheaply
The cheapest way to test an idea is rarely to build it. Landing pages, clickable
prototypes, concierge tests, and fake-door experiments can validate demand and
usability for a fraction of the cost of full delivery. Discovery is about learning
fast and cheap.

## The Opportunity Solution Tree
Teresa Torres's opportunity solution tree links a desired outcome to the
opportunities (unmet needs, pains, desires) that could drive it, then to candidate
solutions, then to experiments. It keeps solutions traceable to a real outcome and
prevents feature factories.
