# What Is a Product Requirements Document (PRD)?

## Definition
A Product Requirements Document (PRD) is a written specification that describes
what a product or feature should do, who it is for, and why it is worth building.
It is the shared source of truth that aligns product, engineering, design, and
business stakeholders on the definition of done before implementation begins.

## Purpose
A PRD answers three questions: what problem are we solving, for whom, and how
will we know we succeeded. It translates a product strategy into concrete,
buildable requirements. A good PRD reduces ambiguity, prevents rework, and lets a
team estimate, sequence, and test work with confidence. It is a communication
tool first and a specification second.

## What a PRD Is Not
A PRD is not a technical design document; it states requirements and outcomes, not
implementation details such as database schemas or class hierarchies (those belong
in an engineering design doc or RFC). It is not a project plan or Gantt chart, and
it is not a marketing brief. It focuses on the "what" and "why," leaving most of
the "how" to engineering.

## When to Write One
Write a PRD when the work is large or ambiguous enough that people could
reasonably disagree about scope, when multiple teams must coordinate, or when the
cost of building the wrong thing is high. Small, well-understood changes may only
need a ticket. The heavier the coordination and the higher the risk, the more a
PRD pays for itself.

## Living Document
A PRD is a living document. It should be versioned, dated, and updated as the team
learns. Open questions are expected early and should shrink over time. A PRD that
never changes after kickoff is usually being ignored.

## Ownership
The product manager owns the PRD, but it is written collaboratively. Engineering
validates feasibility, design shapes the user experience, and stakeholders confirm
the business case. The PM is accountable for keeping it coherent and current.
