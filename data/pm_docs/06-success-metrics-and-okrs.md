# Success Metrics and OKRs

## Why Metrics Belong in a PRD
A PRD without success metrics cannot be evaluated. Metrics define what winning looks
like and let the team decide, after launch, whether to double down, iterate, or kill
the feature. Every objective should have at least one measurable signal.

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators
Lagging indicators (revenue, retention, churn) confirm outcomes but move slowly.
Leading indicators (activation rate, time-to-first-value, feature adoption) move
early and predict the lagging ones. Good PRDs track both: leading metrics to steer
during the build and early launch, lagging metrics to judge lasting impact.

## The HEART Framework
Google's HEART framework offers five dimensions for user-experience metrics:
Happiness (satisfaction, NPS), Engagement (frequency, depth of use), Adoption (new
users of a feature), Retention (users who return), and Task success (completion
rate, error rate, time on task). For each, pick a Goal, a Signal, and a Metric.

## OKRs
Objectives and Key Results pair a qualitative, inspirational Objective with three to
five quantitative Key Results that measure progress. A strong Objective is
memorable and directional ("Make onboarding effortless"); strong Key Results are
measurable and outcome-based ("Increase day-7 retention from 30% to 45%"), not task
lists. Key Results measure results, not activity.

## Counter-Metrics and Guardrails
Every primary metric should have a guardrail that prevents gaming it. If you push
for engagement, watch for spam or fatigue; if you push for signups, watch activation
quality. Guardrail metrics keep optimization honest.

## Setting Baselines and Targets
State the current baseline, the target, and the time window. "Increase conversion"
is not a metric; "Increase checkout conversion from 2.1% to 3.0% within one quarter"
is. Without a baseline you cannot tell whether you succeeded.

## Avoid Vanity Metrics
Vanity metrics (raw page views, total registered users) look good but do not inform
decisions. Prefer actionable, comparable, ratio-based metrics that tie to user value
and business outcomes.
